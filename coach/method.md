# NORWEGIAN SINGLES APPROACH (NSA) — 4–5 DAY ADAPTATION
*Sustainable Sub-Threshold Training for Hobby Joggers Training 4–5x/Week*

---

## METHODOLOGY OVERVIEW

The Norwegian Singles Approach (NSA) is a time-efficient application of Norwegian training principles. Unlike elite "double threshold" methods, this approach focuses on **one quality session per day** ("singles") to maximize aerobic development without the injury risk or burnout of high-intensity anaerobic work.

**This document adapts the method to a 4–5 day/week schedule.** The canonical NSA is built for 6–7 days with **3** sub-threshold sessions. No primary source publishes a sub-6-day protocol, so the structure below is **derived from the method's core principles** rather than copied from a published plan. The key adaptation is well-supported: at reduced frequency, **drop from 3 quality sessions to 2** (the only documented reduced-load lever in the original method).

### Core Framework

1.  **2 Quality + Easy + Long**: 2 Sub-Threshold sessions per week, the rest easy running, plus one long run.
2.  **Preserve the Ratio by Time**: Keep sub-threshold work at ~**20–25% of weekly running time**, the rest (~75–80%) easy. This ratio defines the method and holds at any frequency.
3.  **Controlled Intensity**: Quality work is strictly **Sub-Threshold** (below the 4.0 mmol/L "threshold"), not "hard" threshold.
4.  **Flat Loading**: Replaces "boom-bust" periodization with a consistent, repeatable weekly load.
5.  **Strict Easy Discipline**: Easy runs must be truly easy to allow the 2 quality days to be absorbed.

> **Note on evidence:** The 2-quality / 4–5 day structure, the pace-vs-HR control logic, and the "minimize but don't eliminate" stance on VO2max all reflect cross-source consensus (Bakken's original material, norwegiansingles.run, the LGTIT papers, peer-reviewed physiology). The "flat load → week-9 breakthrough" timeline and the strict no-periodization model are **practitioner lore that the research did not substantiate** — treat them as a sensible default, not a proven law.

## PHYSIOLOGICAL TARGETS

The goal is to accumulate time in the "sweet spot" to push the aerobic threshold up, rather than stressing the anaerobic system.

| Zone | Metric | Feeling | Goal |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Easy** | **< 70% Max HR (HR ceiling)** | Conversational, nasal breathing | Recovery, volume accumulation |
| **Sub-Threshold** | **~2.5 – 3.5 mmol/L** (below 4.0) | Comfortably hard, controlled | Maximize aerobic load without accumulating fatigue |
| **Anaerobic** | **> 4.0 mmol/L** | Burning legs, gasping | **Minimized / deferred** (not part of the base phase) |

**On the lactate band:** The exact sub-threshold target is genuinely contested across sources — Bakken's original **2.3–3.0 mmol/L** (lower for fitter athletes), the practitioner community **2.5–3.5 mmol/L**, and the academic LGTIT range a broader **2–4.5 mmol/L**. The unifying consensus is simply: **comfortably below 4.0, and individualized.** The 2.5–3.5 figure used here is the community sweet spot.

**Key Differentiator:** This method **minimizes** VO2max intervals, neuromuscular speed work, and hill repeats so the aerobic system is the primary stimulus — it does **not** ban them outright (see "The X Element" below). For a 4–5 day hobby runner, defer them until progress clearly stalls.

## WEEKLY STRUCTURE

Use a repetitive "flat week." Quality sessions are **never run back-to-back** — keep 48–72h between them. Pick the layout that matches your available days.

### 5-Day Week (primary)

| Day | Session Type | Duration | Protocol |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Mon** | Easy Run | 45–60 min | HR ceiling < 70% Max |
| **Tue** | **Sub-Threshold (A)** | 50–60 min | Shorter reps (e.g. 8–12 x 1000m) |
| **Wed** | Easy Run | 45–60 min | HR ceiling < 70% Max |
| **Thu** | **Sub-Threshold (B)** | 50–60 min | Longer reps (e.g. 4–6 x 2000m or 3 x 3000m) |
| **Fri** | Rest | — | — |
| **Sat** | **Long Run** | 75–90 min | Easy pace (HR may drift slightly at the end) |
| **Sun** | Rest | — | — |

### 4-Day Week (minimum)

| Day | Session Type | Duration | Protocol |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Tue** | **Sub-Threshold (A)** | 50–60 min | Shorter reps (e.g. 8–12 x 1000m) |
| **Thu** | Easy Run | 45–60 min | HR ceiling < 70% Max |
| **Sat** | **Sub-Threshold (B)** | 50–60 min | Longer reps (e.g. 4–6 x 2000m or 3 x 3000m) |
| **Sun** | **Long Run** | 75–90 min | Easy pace |

**Rotation:** Alternate the format of the two quality days week to week so you cycle through short (1K), medium (2K), and long (3K) reps over time. Total weekly sub-threshold volume matters more than which exact format you run on a given day.

**Scaling up:** Only add a 3rd quality session if you move back toward a 6-day week. At 4–5 days, two is the correct ceiling.

## SESSION PROTOCOLS

### Controlling Intensity: Pace Target, HR Ceiling

This is a correction to the common "run by HR, not pace" advice. Peer-reviewed physiology shows that over a single session **HR drifts upward (~+4% over 40 min) while actual aerobic effort stays flat**, and that a fixed pace produces very different internal loads between individuals. So no single number is the gold standard — **triangulate**:

* **Pace = the working in-run target.** Derive sub-threshold pace from a recent test or race result. This is your primary guide rep-to-rep. Adjust it **faster for shorter reps, slower for longer reps**, and slower in heat/humidity/fatigue.
* **HR = a ceiling, not a target.** Use it to catch yourself going too hard (don't blow past your Z4 cap), not to chase a number — especially late in a session when drift sets in.
* **RPE / feel = the sanity check.** Sub-threshold should feel "comfortably hard, controlled" — you could do 2–3 more reps at the end.
* **Lactate = gold standard, if you own a meter.** Most hobbyists won't; pace + HR ceiling + feel is the practical substitute.

### 1. Sub-Threshold Intervals
*Target: ~20–25% of weekly running time.*

Interval-based, not continuous tempo. Rest length is **not critical** — Bakken himself said "the pause isn't so important." Total volume at the right intensity is what counts.

* **Short Intervals (1K):**
    * **Format:** 8–12 x 1000m
    * **Rest:** ~60s standing/walking (anything 60s–3min is fine)
    * **Feel:** Comfortably hard. Could do 2–3 more reps at the end.

* **Medium Intervals (2K):**
    * **Format:** 4–6 x 2000m
    * **Rest:** 60–90s
    * **Feel:** Controlled, identical effort across all reps.

* **Long Intervals (3K / 10min):**
    * **Format:** 3 x 3000m, or 3 x 10min
    * **Rest:** 90s
    * **Feel:** Sustained tempo.

> Published exact paces and "float" recovery formats are **illustrative, not prescriptive** — the specific pace-and-float fartlek format was not confirmed as canonical. Run the controlled sub-threshold effort; don't fixate on a copied pace.

### 2. Easy Runs
*Target: ~75–80% of weekly running time.*

* **Intensity:** Keep HR under ~70% of Max (treat as a ceiling).
* **Pace:** Whatever keeps HR under the ceiling. If HR is low, the run is easy regardless of speed.
* **Purpose:** Recover for the next quality day. Run these too fast and the method fails — you'll carry fatigue into the sub-threshold sessions.

### 3. The "X Element" (optional, deferred)
The original method is **not** purely sub-threshold: Bakken retained one weekly higher-intensity session (~5–10 mmol/L, often hills) — the "X element" — and the elite model keeps one VO2max session/week. For a 4–5 day hobbyist, **leave this out by default.** Reintroduce a small amount (e.g. strides, or one short hill/VO2max session) only when progress clearly stalls, and not at the cost of a quality day's quality.

## EXECUTION & PROGRESSION

### The "Flat Load" Philosophy
Instead of building for 3 weeks and recovering for 1, find a load you can repeat indefinitely.
* **Week 1–2:** Focus on completion. Run slower than you think you need to.
* **Week 3–8 (The Doubt Phase):** You may feel stagnant. **Do not increase intensity.** Aerobic adaptations are slow.
* **Week 9+:** Many practitioners report a "breakthrough" where the same effort yields a faster pace.

> *Caveat: the specific 6–8 week timeline and the strict no-periodization model are practitioner experience, not validated findings. Use them as a default expectation, not a guarantee.*

### Marathon Adjustments (12 Weeks Out)
The 4–5 day structure scales to the marathon with minimal changes:
1.  **Long Run:** Gradually extend toward ~2–2.5 hours.
2.  **Specific Intensity:** Convert one Sub-Threshold session (the longer-rep day) to Marathon Pace work (e.g. 5 x 5000m @ Goal MP).
3.  **Volume:** Total hours may increase slightly, but the structure stays flat and stays at 2 quality sessions.

## COMMON ERRORS TO AVOID

1.  **Adding a 3rd quality day at 4–5 days/week:** Two sub-threshold sessions is the ceiling at this frequency. A third only belongs in a 6–7 day week.
2.  **Running Easy Runs too Fast:** If easy runs creep toward 75% HR, you carry fatigue into the quality sessions and fail to absorb the training.
3.  **Chasing HR as a precise target:** HR drifts within a session. Use pace as the in-run target and HR as a ceiling — not the reverse.
4.  **Turning it into 80/20 with hard speed work:** Keep the quality strictly sub-threshold. Defer the optional X element until progress stalls.
5.  **Stacking quality back-to-back:** Keep 48–72h between the two sub-threshold sessions.
6.  **Judging by "Suffering":** Workouts should not leave you destroyed. Success = being able to repeat the session 48h later, not exhaustion.

> **Mantra:** *"Find the highest training load you can handle indefinitely, then back off 10% so you're not constantly on the edge."*
